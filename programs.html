Our programs

From awareness to recovery, one continuous pathway.

Every part of our program exists to answer one question: what does a young person in Ethiopia actually need in order to get real, confidential, affordable mental health care? Our answer is not a single service, but a connected pathway — one that meets a student long before they would ever think to search for a psychiatrist, and stays with them through to recovery.

This page sets out how that pathway works in practice: how we reach students, how screening and onboarding happen, what a clinical session actually looks like, how the Treatment Access Subsidy removes cost as a barrier, and the governance that keeps every part of this accountable and safe.

The evidence

A crisis that is well documented and largely unaddressed.

Our programs are not built on assumption. Ethiopia's youth mental health crisis has been documented across multiple peer-reviewed studies and international health bodies, and the figures below are the evidence base that shapes every decision we make — from which conditions we prioritize to how we structure our clinical capacity.

Three compounding barriers

These numbers are not the result of one single failure — they come from three barriers that reinforce each other. Understanding them is what shaped the design of our three pillars.

S

Stigma

Fear of judgment is consistently identified as the primary reason Ethiopian youth avoid seeking mental health care, often preferring silence over the risk of being labeled by peers, family, or their community.

C

Cost

Private psychiatric consultation is priced well beyond what most students can afford, while public facilities are overwhelmed. For low-income families, the choice is often between care and basic necessities.

A

Availability

With so few psychiatrists nationally, specialists are concentrated in a handful of major cities — leaving peri-urban and rural youth with virtually no access to any professional support at all.

Who we serve

Adolescents and young adults, at the moments that matter most.

Our programs are designed for young people aged 14 to 25 — a period that spans the final years of secondary school, the transition into higher education or work, and the early years of independent adult life. It is also the period in which most lifetime mental health conditions first emerge, and the period in which Ethiopian youth are least likely to have anyone checking in on how they're actually doing.

We work with students in both secondary schools and universities, recognizing that the pressures at each stage are different — academic performance and family expectation in secondary school; independence, financial pressure, and identity formation at university — but the barriers to care remain the same at both.

Where we work

Addis Ababa, and the communities around it.

Our outreach currently focuses on Addis Ababa and nearby communities, where we can build direct relationships with school administrations, deliver in-person sessions reliably, and maintain close contact with the Mental Health Clubs we help establish. As our model is proven and our clinical capacity grows, we plan to extend this same approach — outreach paired with telemedicine — to additional Ethiopian regions.

Because clinical care is delivered through our telemedicine platform rather than requiring students to travel to a clinic, our treatment capacity is not limited to wherever we've physically visited — a student who has been through outreach and screening can continue their care entirely online, wherever they are.

In detail

Three pillars, working together

Each pillar below solves a distinct problem. Outreach solves the problem of "students don't know help exists, or are afraid to ask." Telemedicine solves the problem of "help exists, but isn't private, affordable, or nearby." The Subsidy solves the problem of "the referred care itself costs more than a family can pay." Together, they remove every barrier we identified above, one at a time.

01

Community Outreach & Awareness

Outreach is where the relationship with every student begins. Our team visits secondary schools, colleges, and universities and delivers a structured session on mental health literacy: what depression, anxiety, and adjustment difficulties actually look like, how they differ from ordinary stress, and — critically — that seeking help is not a sign of weakness. These sessions are deliberately designed to reduce stigma in the room itself, not just convey information.

A typical session combines a facilitated talk from our outreach staff with a guest clinician available on-site to answer questions directly — because hearing "it's okay to struggle" from a licensed professional carries a different weight than hearing it from a pamphlet. Students are given space to ask questions anonymously, including questions they might never ask in front of a teacher or parent.

Outreach also does something less visible but equally important: it identifies who needs support right now. With informed consent, our team offers validated screening — including the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 — in a semi-private setting, never in a way that exposes a student's responses to their classmates. Students who screen as needing further support are given a clear, low-friction path into our telemedicine platform.

Finally, at every partner institution we help establish a student-led Mental Health Club. This is what sustains the impact of a single outreach visit over an entire school year: a peer network that keeps mental health conversations alive between our visits, reduces stigma from within the student body itself, and gives students an ongoing, familiar point of contact if they ever need to be pointed toward help.

FormatIn-person workshops at partner schools and universities, supported by a licensed guest clinician on-site.
ScreeningValidated tools (PHQ-9, GAD-7), administered with informed consent, in a semi-private setting.
ConsentWritten parental consent is required for any student under 18 before screening takes place.
Follow-throughA Mental Health Club at each institution keeps awareness alive between outreach visits.
02

Telemedicine-Enabled Clinical Care

Once a student is identified as needing further support, they are introduced to our in-house telemedicine platform — built specifically around the reality that the young Ethiopians who most need mental health care are also the most afraid to be seen seeking it. Every design decision in the platform starts from that insight.

Registration is anonymous by design. No real name, no photograph, and no institution identifier is required to create an account. Once registered, a student is matched with a licensed psychiatrist and assigned an anonymized Case ID — the only identifier that ever appears in clinical notes or session records. Clinical staff never attempt to learn a patient's real identity beyond what is clinically necessary.

Sessions are delivered by encrypted voice or video call, and the choice between the two belongs entirely to the patient — some students are more comfortable being heard than seen, and the platform is built to support either without friction. Session frequency is tailored to clinical need, with regular check-ins for students in active care and the flexibility to adjust as a student's needs change over time.

For students who prefer face-to-face contact, or whose situation calls for an in-person assessment, we also offer weekend clinic hours at our Addis Ababa office — a quiet, confidential space entirely separate from any school or university setting, so that attending a session never has to mean explaining to anyone where you're going.

Every clinician on our platform works under the supervision of a senior Clinical Supervisor, and every case involving risk of harm is handled through a defined escalation protocol — including contacting a registered emergency contact and, where necessary, referral to Ethiopia's emergency services or specialized psychiatric facilities. No student is ever left to manage a crisis alone.

AnonymityPatients are identified only by an anonymized Case ID, visible solely to their treating clinician.
ModalityEncrypted voice or video, chosen by the patient — with in-person Saturday sessions also available.
Session modelFrequency tailored to clinical need, reviewed and adjusted as treatment progresses.
EscalationA defined crisis protocol, supervised by a senior Clinical Supervisor, for any case involving risk of harm.
03

Treatment Access Subsidy

Counselling and psychiatric consultation solve part of the problem — but many patients are eventually referred for prescribed medication or specialist care beyond what our platform provides, and for underprivileged youth, that referral can be where progress stops entirely. A prescription that a family cannot afford is functionally the same as no prescription at all.

The Treatment Access Subsidy exists to close exactly that gap. It is a means-tested fund that covers the cost of prescribed medication and externally referred clinical treatment for eligible patients — activated only once a licensed external medical practitioner has made a referral and issued a prescription. Safe Minds Ethiopia does not diagnose, treat, or prescribe medication ourselves; our clinical scope is strictly psychosocial counselling and psychiatric consultation, and the Subsidy respects that boundary by design.

In practice, this means a student's care does not stall at the point where it becomes expensive. A referral is followed by a subsidy application, reviewed against eligibility criteria, and — where approved — the cost that would otherwise fall on the family is covered directly. All subsidy payments are recorded in a dedicated register and reviewed quarterly, so that the fund remains transparent and fully accountable to the donors who make it possible.

EligibilityMeans-tested, and activated only upon a signed referral and valid prescription from a licensed external practitioner.
ScopeCovers referred medication and treatment costs — Safe Minds Ethiopia does not diagnose or prescribe.
PurposeEnsures "I cannot afford it" is never the reason a young person goes without treatment.
AccountabilityEvery payment is logged in a dedicated subsidy register and reviewed by the Board quarterly.

How it all connects

The pathway every student follows

No single service on its own closes Ethiopia's treatment gap. What matters is that a student who walks into an outreach session in the morning can, if they need to, be in a confidential conversation with a licensed psychiatrist within days — without ever having to explain themselves to a parent, teacher, or classmate along the way.

Measuring impact

We track the journey, not just a single number.

No single statistic tells the full story of what happens to a young person who comes through our program. So instead of reporting one headline figure, we track each stage of the journey — from the first moment a student hears about mental health at an outreach session, through screening and treatment, to measurable improvement and eventual recovery.

Students reached Tracked through event attendance records and partner institution reports at every outreach session.
Intake assessments completed Measured using PHQ-9 and other validated screening tools administered with informed consent.
Patients in ongoing treatment Tracked through anonymized platform session records — never linked to a student's real identity in reporting.
Clinical sessions delivered Drawn from platform usage data, reviewed by the Clinical Supervisor as part of monthly oversight.
Mental Health Clubs established Confirmed directly with each partner institution as clubs are formed and become active.
Change in symptom scores Assessed by comparing a patient's baseline PHQ-9/GAD-7 score against follow-up assessments over the course of treatment.
Treatment completion Tracked through platform outcome data, distinguishing patients who complete their initial course of care from those who disengage early.
Patient-reported satisfaction Collected through a short, anonymous survey offered after sessions, used to improve clinical quality over time.

Clinical governance

Care delivered within a clearly defined scope.

Safe Minds Ethiopia operates strictly within a psychosocial counselling and psychiatric consultation scope. We provide support for depression, adjustment difficulties, early-stage substance use concerns, childhood trauma symptoms, and general student counselling needs — the conditions our evidence base shows are both highly prevalent among Ethiopian youth and severely underserved.

We do not diagnose or manage severe psychiatric conditions such as psychosis or bipolar disorder, and we do not handle acute psychiatric emergencies. Every case that falls outside this scope is referred immediately to licensed medical or emergency services through an established, documented referral pathway — including named partner facilities equipped to provide the higher level of care the situation requires.

This boundary is not a limitation we apologize for — it is what allows us to deliver the care we do provide safely, consistently, and to a professional standard, rather than attempting to be all things to all patients.

Clinical Supervisor A senior licensed psychiatrist provides monthly individual supervision and quarterly group case review for every clinician on our platform, and reviews all high-risk cases directly.
Safeguarding Lead Responsible for protecting every beneficiary, with particular attention to users under eighteen, who require verified parental consent before any service begins — including screening, counselling, or platform registration.
Data protection All session content and clinical records are end-to-end encrypted, stored under an anonymized Case ID, and access is restricted strictly by role — never shared with schools, families, or employers without explicit consent.
Crisis protocol Any disclosure of risk to self or others triggers a defined, immediate response — contacting a registered emergency contact and, where needed, referral to emergency services or a specialized psychiatric facility.
